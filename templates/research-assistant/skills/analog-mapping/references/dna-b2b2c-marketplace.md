# DNA Extraction Template: B2B2C Marketplace

Use when the seed company sits between an institutional payer (B-side) and an individual
end user (C-side), where the B-side pays but the C-side consumes. The company does not
produce the underlying product — it connects institutional demand (employer, insurer,
government, institution) to supply (programs, services, goods) on behalf of individuals.

Key signal: The C-side would not buy this on their own, or would buy a meaningfully
inferior version. The B-side pays because it serves their interests (retention, compliance,
cost reduction, talent) — not primarily as pure altruism.

---

## The Seven Dimensions (B2B2C Lens)

### 1. B-Side Payer Structure
- Who specifically pays on the B-side — which role, which budget?
- What is the B-side's stated motivation (employee retention, compliance, cost reduction,
  talent attraction, regulatory requirement)?
- What is the switching cost for the B-side? What does it take to churn?
- How is B-side pricing structured — per-employee, per-engagement, platform fee,
  take rate on C-side spend, or outcome-based?
- What is the B-side's alternative: build internally, buy from a competitor, do nothing?

### 2. C-Side Consumer Structure
- Who consumes on the C-side, and how are they reached — through the B-side only,
  through direct marketing, or through network/community effects?
- Does the C-side have any awareness they are using a B-funded platform, or is it
  seamless/invisible?
- What is the C-side's switching cost? Can they access the same supply elsewhere?
- Does C-side engagement affect B-side willingness to pay (outcomes-linked pricing)?

### 3. Supply Side
- Who provides the underlying product or service that C-side users consume?
- Is supply proprietary (company-produced) or aggregated (third-party providers)?
- How concentrated is supply? Can suppliers go direct to C-side and bypass the platform?
- What leverage does the platform have over supply — volume, demand aggregation,
  data, branding, financial structuring?
- Does supply quality improve as the platform scales?

### 4. Take Rate Structure
- What percentage of what transaction does the company take?
- From which side — B-side only, C-side only, or both?
- What determines the floor (competition, B-side price sensitivity, regulatory ceiling)?
- What determines the ceiling (value delivered relative to B-side alternatives)?
- How does take rate evolve as the company scales — compress or expand?
- What is the blended gross margin after supply-side costs?

### 5. Network and Flywheel Effects
- Is there a B→C flywheel: more B-side employers → more C-side users → more supply
  leverage → better programs → more B-side wins?
- Is there a data flywheel: more C-side engagement → better outcomes data → more
  compelling B-side ROI story → easier B-side sales?
- Can C-side users benefit from other C-side users (community, peer learning, cohorts)?
- At what scale do network effects become durable vs. fragile?

### 6. Failure Modes Specific to B2B2C
- **B-side disintermediation:** B-side builds the capability internally or buys supply
  direct, cutting out the platform
- **C-side apathy:** C-side doesn't engage, making B-side ROI story impossible to prove
- **Supply commoditization:** Supply side standardizes and B-side or C-side sources it
  directly at lower cost
- **B-side consolidation:** A larger B-side platform (HRIS, benefits admin, ERP) absorbs
  the category as a feature
- **Regulatory intervention:** Government mandates the benefit or regulates the take rate
- **C-side disillusionment:** C-side experiences poor outcomes, undermining B-side's
  willingness to continue paying

### 7. Scale Dependencies
- What B-side density is required for the supply side to take the platform seriously?
- What C-side engagement rate is required to make the B-side ROI story credible?
- What data volume is required for the outcomes moat to be defensible?
- Is there a geographic density constraint (needs employers and supply in the same region)?

**Supply side:** [Who, proprietary vs. aggregated, supplier leverage/concentration]

## Search Heuristics for B2B2C Analogs

When searching for analogs, look for:

- **Same institutional-payer structure in different verticals:** Who else has an employer,
  insurer, government, or institution paying for an individual's benefit? Healthcare
  navigation, FSA/HSA administration, commuter benefits, employee assistance programs,
  childcare subsidies, legal insurance, wellness platforms.

- **Same supply-aggregation model:** Who else aggregates third-party supply (not
  proprietary) to serve institutionally-funded individual consumption? Benefits
  administrators, pharmacy benefit managers, managed care networks.

- **Same take rate tension:** Who else negotiates between an institutional payer (price
  sensitive, focused on aggregate cost) and a fragmented supply side (program quality
  matters to C-side)? Insurance intermediaries, GPOs (group purchasing organizations).

- **Same outcomes-linked value proposition:** Who else is paid by an institution because
  they can prove individual outcomes — not just access? Find where ROI proof matters.

## What NOT to Search For

- Direct competitors (companies doing the same thing in the same domain)
- Pure B2B (no individual end user)
- Pure B2C (no institutional payer — individual pays directly)
- Vertical software that serves B but doesn't touch C
