# Crossing the Chasm: Geoffrey Moore's Adoption Lifecycle

## Core Question
**How do we move from early adopters to mainstream? What's the bowling pin strategy?**

Geoffrey Moore's Crossing the Chasm explains why revolutionary products often stall after early success. The insight: early adopters and early majority have fundamentally different buying criteria. There's a chasm between them. Falling into the chasm kills startups.

---

## The Technology Adoption Lifecycle

### 1. Innovators (2.5%)
**Who:** Love new technology, high risk tolerance, "early geeks"

**What they want:** Cool, novel, cutting-edge — regardless of maturity

**Why they buy:** Because it's new and interesting

**Example:** Early technology adopters in the target market

### 2. Early Adopters (13.5%)
**Who:** Visionary customers, willing to take risks, driven by potential

**What they want:** Competitive advantage, transformation, "we could do something different"

**Why they buy:** See a vision of what could be; want to be first-movers; willing to tolerate rough edges

**Example:** Early adopters in the target market willing to experiment with new approaches

### 3. THE CHASM [You Are Here]
**The gap where momentum dies.**

Early adopters and Early Majority have *opposite* criteria. A product optimized for early adopters fails with early majority.

**Early Adopter buying criteria:**
- Vision of transformation (even if execution is incomplete)
- Can tolerate immaturity, missing features, risk
- Wants proof of concept, not proven solution
- Buys from individuals with vision (founders, visionaries)

**Early Majority buying criteria:**
- Proven solution (others have done this and succeeded)
- Low risk, reliable, well-supported
- Wants references and evidence, not vision
- Buys from established vendors, not startups
- Incremental improvement, not transformation

**Why the chasm exists:**
Early majority sees the company's early success with one segment and thinks "interesting, but this isn't ready for mainstream adoption. They have limited track record. I'll wait for a proven solution or a safer vendor."

### 4. Early Majority (34%)
**Who:** Pragmatists, slower to adopt but represent mainstream

**What they want:** Proven, reliable, supported, references from similar companies

**Why they buy:** "If it works for companies like us, it can work for us. Lower risk."

**Example:** Mainstream market customers requiring proven solutions and track records

### 5. Late Majority (34%)
**Who:** Skeptics, only buy when a standard exists and social pressure mounts

**What they want:** Commodity, standardized, no learning curve, everyone else is using it

**Why they buy:** "Everyone does it this way; we have to follow." Risk of being left behind.

### 6. Laggards (16%)
**Who:** Resistant to change, buy only when legacy solutions disappear

## The Critical Transition: Crossing the Chasm

### Why Startups Stall in the Chasm

You've got early adopters loving your product. Revenue is growing, they're evangelizing. But:

1. **Early adopters are not early majority** — They're 13% of market, and they're satisfied. Trying to grow into early majority with early-adopter marketing doesn't work.

2. **Early majority sees risk, not vision** — They look at your company and see "unproven startup" + "early-stage references" + "missing some features." They think, "I'll wait for an established vendor to adopt this."

3. **Messaging gap** — Messaging that works for early adopters ("Transform hiring with transparency") doesn't work for early majority ("Reduce hiring risk with proven, reliable matching").

4. **Sales gap** — You're selling vision to people who want risk reduction. Two different sales processes.

5. **Product gap** — Product built for early adopters (flexible, experimental) isn't what early majority wants (stable, integrated, supported).

### How to Cross the Chasm: The Bowling Pin Strategy

The insight: Don't try to go after the entire early majority. Pick a beachhead segment first.

**Bowling pin strategy:**
1. **Pick one pin (beachhead segment)**
2. **Dominate that pin completely**
3. **Use it to knock over adjacent pins**
4. **Eventually knock over the whole market**

**Why this works:** You create a proven reference in one segment. Early majority in adjacent segments see your success and follow.

**Potential beachhead options:**

- Analyze the segments where early adopters are concentrated
- Identify which segments have the scale, accessibility, and referenceable nature needed
- Select the segment that will create credibility for expansion to adjacent markets

### How to Know Which Beachhead to Pick

**The beachhead test:**
1. **Accessible market** — Can you reach them? Can you sell to them without channel (direct sales)?
2. **Shared problem** — Do they have a common, acute problem that you solve?
3. **Reference-able** — Once you win them, will they become credible references for other segments?
4. **Winnable** — Can you credibly compete? Can you beat their current solution?

## Post-Chasm: Scaling from Early Majority to Late Majority

Once you've crossed and have references in your beachhead:

1. **Expand to adjacent segments** — "Segment A uses us successfully, so segment B should too"
2. **Transition messaging** — From "Transform hiring" (early adopter) to "Reduce risk, proven results" (early majority)
3. **Mature product** — Add integrations, support, stability that early majority requires
4. **Build standard** — Create industry momentum ("This is how modern hiring works")

By the time late majority enters, you're the standard.

## Common Mistakes Crossing the Chasm

### Mistake 1: Trying to cross with the whole market at once
You try to scale with bootcamps, universities, employers, and learners simultaneously. None get the focus needed to win. Result: stalled growth.

**Fix:** Pick ONE beachhead. Win it completely before expanding.

### Mistake 2: Optimizing product for early adopters, then surprised when early majority doesn't buy
You build flexibility and experimental features that early adopters love. Early majority wants stability and integration. You now have the wrong product.

**Fix:** Once you've crossed, the product needs to change. This is why crossing often requires a "Version 2."

### Mistake 3: Messaging stays in early-adopter language ("Transform," "Revolutionary")
Early majority is pragmatic. They don't want revolution; they want risk reduction and proven results.

**Fix:** Marketing has to change. You need case studies, references, messaging about reliability.

### Mistake 4: Sales model doesn't change
Early adopters respond to founder pitches and vision. Early majority wants structured sales (RFP, references, established process).

**Fix:** Move from founder-led to sales team; from vision-selling to reference-selling.

### Mistake 5: Not recognizing when you've successfully crossed
You achieved early majority adoption in one segment and didn't notice. You're still optimizing for early adopters while a new market is ready.

**Fix:** Monitor adoption curves per segment. Recognize successful crossing and adjust strategy accordingly.

## Diagnostics: Are We in the Chasm?

**Signs you're in the chasm:**
- Early adopters are happy; new customer growth is slowing or flat
- Sales cycle is stretching (prospects ask for references, integrations, support)
- Competitive pressure from bigger vendors is increasing
- Product is getting requests for "boring" stuff (integration, stability, support), not "cool" stuff
- You have customers in one segment but can't crack adjacent segments

**Signs you've crossed:**
- Growth inflecting upward again (not just from early adopters buying)
- References are working (prospects say "yes" when they hear from similar companies)
- Sales cycle is predictable (not every deal requires founder involvement)
- TAM is clearly expanding beyond early adopters
