# 7 Powers: Hamilton Helmer's Framework

## Core Question
**What is our durable competitive advantage, and which power(s) create it?**

Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers framework identifies the specific sources of lasting competitive advantage. Not all advantages are durable — many are temporary. This framework helps you diagnose which of the 7 powers you actually possess, which you're missing, and how defensible your position really is.

## The 7 Powers

### 1. Scale Economies
**Definition:** Your cost per unit decreases as you produce more volume.

**What it looks like:**
- Fixed costs spread across more units
- Supplier discounts from larger volume purchases
- Learning curve improvements from manufacturing scale
- Network effects that grow with scale

**How to test for it:**
- Do our costs materially decline as we grow? (Calculate cost per unit at different volumes)
- Can a smaller competitor match our unit costs? (Usually no, if true scale economies exist)
- Are the economies real cost reductions, or just operational efficiencies? (Distinguish between sustainable and temporary)
- What's our minimum efficient scale? (Below this, the advantage disappears)

**Common mistakes:**
- Confusing variable cost improvements with scale economies (they're different)
- Assuming scale economies exist without calculating them
- Overestimating how much smaller competitors are disadvantaged at scale

### 2. Network Effects
**Definition:** The product becomes more valuable as more people use it — and this creates a self-reinforcing cycle.

**Types:**
- **Direct:** More users → product is more valuable (e.g., LinkedIn, Slack)
- **Indirect:** More users → attracts complementary products/suppliers → attracts more users (e.g., Windows, iOS)
- **Two-sided:** Growth on one side drives growth on the other (e.g., marketplace, platform)

**How to test for it:**
- Does the product get objectively more valuable as users grow? (Not just subjectively better marketing)
- Do you see new users adopt faster in concentrated regions? (Geographic network effect test)
- Can a new competitor with zero users compete effectively? (If not, network effects are strong)
- Do users churn when the network shrinks? (The reverse test)

**Common mistakes:**
- Confusing "more users make it better" with network effects (better matching ≠ network effects)
- Assuming two-sided platforms automatically have strong network effects (many don't)
- Overweighting weak network effects as a moat

### 3. Counter-Positioning
**Definition:** You do something your competitors can't do because their legacy business model would be destroyed if they did it.

**What it looks like:**
- Disruption from below: You compete on different dimensions they've abandoned
- Different cost structure or business model
- Competitors rationally choose not to compete with you
- Often paired with Disruptive Innovation

**How to test for it:**
- What would it cost our competitors to match our positioning? (Not just money, but internal disruption)
- Would matching us destroy their existing business? (If yes, true counter-positioning)
- Could they do it if they wanted to? (The key test — incumbents often *can't*, not just *won't*)
- Are they ignoring us because of strategy or because they're trapped? (Helps distinguish from just being outmaneuvered)

**Common mistakes:**
- Confusing product differentiation with counter-positioning (just being different isn't enough)
- Assuming competitors won't adapt (they often do, slowly but eventually)
- Overweighting this power without validating that the competitor is actually trapped

### 4. Switching Costs
**Definition:** It's expensive for customers to leave you (money, time, effort, organizational resistance).

**What it looks like:**
- Data integration and customization (switching requires migrating data and retraining)
- Switching penalties (contractual lock-in, learning curve, institutional muscle memory)
- Switching benefits aren't compelling enough to justify the cost
- Lock-in grows over time as customers invest more

**How to test for it:**
- How much would a customer spend to switch to a competitor? (Money, time, organizational overhead)
- How many customers have left? And why? (If they leave easily, switching costs are low)
- Do you lose customers primarily to competitor switching or just churn? (Switching costs show up in low competitor conversion)
- What happens when a competitor offers 20% better functionality? (Do customers switch?)

**Company-Specific Application:**

**Common mistakes:**
- Overestimating contractual lock-in as a durable advantage (contracts can be broken)
- Confusing customer satisfaction with switching costs (happy customers don't leave; that's retention, not switching costs)
- Assuming switching costs apply equally to all customers (SMBs switch easier than enterprises)

### 5. Branding
**Definition:** Customers perceive higher quality or desirability, and this perception allows you to charge premium pricing or win disproportionate share of attention.

**What it looks like:**
- Price premium (you charge more for similar features)
- Disproportionate market share in a commoditized category
- Customer preference despite equivalent alternatives
- Perception of quality/prestige that justifies the premium

**How to test for it:**
- Can you charge 20% more for the same product? (If yes, strong brand power)
- Do customers choose you over cheaper alternatives? (Market share despite price)
- Do you get free marketing (word-of-mouth, media coverage)? (Brand effect)
- Would customers pick your product blind against competitors? (Perception or reality?)

**Common mistakes:**
- Confusing brand awareness with brand power (recognizable ≠ valuable)
- Assuming brand is permanent (it erodes quickly if you violate the promise)
- Overweighting brand without validating willingness to pay

### 6. Cornered Resource
**Definition:** You uniquely own or control a scarce, valuable resource that competitors can't access.

**Types:**
- Exclusive data (you have data competitors can't get)
- Talent (you employ the best people in the category)
- Location (geography, real estate)
- Relationships (exclusive partnerships, institutional trust)
- Government-granted rights (licenses, patents)

**How to test for it:**
- Can competitors realistically get this resource? (Time required, cost, likelihood)
- Is the resource actually scarce, or just currently exclusive? (Growing scarcity = stronger moat)
- How much would it cost a competitor to replicate? (Years? Billions? Impossible?)
- Would losing this resource tank the company? (Strength of dependence)

**Common mistakes:**
- Assuming data is cornered when it's actually replicable (if competitors can gather it, it's not cornered)
- Overweighting current exclusivity without considering time-to-replication
- Mistaking unique-to-you with impossible-for-others

### 7. Process Power
**Definition:** You have proprietary processes, systems, or know-how that competitors can't easily replicate, even if they know they exist.

**What it looks like:**
- Operational excellence (you execute better, faster, cheaper than anyone)
- Proprietary methodologies (hiring algorithms, matching logic, underwriting models)
- Organizational knowledge (implicit, embedded, hard to codify)
- Institutional practices that create consistent advantage

**How to test for it:**
- Have competitors tried to replicate your process? (If yes, did they fail? Why?)
- Is the process dependent on key people, or is it truly institutional? (Key person dependency = weak process power)
- What would it take a competitor to match? (Months? Years? Why that long?)
- Is the process documented and transferable, or implicit? (Implicit = stronger protection, but harder to scale)

**Common mistakes:**
- Confusing operational excellence with process power (being good isn't enough; others must be unable to replicate)
- Overestimating how long it takes competitors to reverse-engineer your process
- Underestimating the role of luck and timing in what appears to be process power

## Diagnostic Exercise: Power Portfolio Assessment

**For each power, answer:**
1. Do we possess this? (Yes / Partially / No)
2. How durable is it? (Years until replicable: <1 / 1-3 / 3-5 / 5+)
3. How does it create defensibility in our specific market?
4. What would it take a competitor to neutralize it?

## Durability KPIs: What to Track for Each Power

Static analysis tells you where you are. These KPIs tell you whether each power is strengthening or eroding over time. Track quarterly.

| Power | KPI | What It Tells You | Watch For |
|-------|-----|-------------------|-----------|
| **Scale Economies** | Cost per learner served (fully loaded) | Whether unit economics improve with volume | Cost per learner flat or rising despite volume growth = scale economies aren't materializing |
| **Scale Economies** | ML model accuracy vs. 6 months ago | Whether data volume is improving predictions | Accuracy plateau = diminishing returns on data scale |
| **Network Effects** | % of employer demand filled organically through the network (vs. manual sourcing) | Whether the network is self-reinforcing | If manual sourcing stays >50% of placements, network effects aren't real — you're running a services business |
| **Network Effects** | Supply-side multi-tenanting rate | Whether suppliers (learning partners, hiring partners) also use competitors | Rising multi-tenanting = leaky bucket; network lock-in weakening |
| **Counter-Positioning** | Incumbent feature parity score (0-100) | Whether incumbents are copying your model | Score rising >60 = counter-positioning window closing; accelerate other powers |
| **Counter-Positioning** | Win rate vs. specific incumbent competitors | Whether positioning advantage translates to deals | Win rate declining = incumbents adapting faster than expected |
| **Switching Costs** | Average data volume per employer (records, integrations, custom workflows) | Whether integration depth is growing | Low data volume after 12+ months = switching costs aren't accumulating |
| **Switching Costs** | Employer churn rate + churn reason | Whether switching costs prevent departure | Churn reason = "found a better product" means switching costs aren't high enough to retain |
| **Branding** | Unaided awareness among target ICPs | Whether the brand exists in buyers' minds | Flat awareness despite marketing spend = brand not breaking through |
| **Branding** | Willingness-to-pay premium (survey or revealed preference) | Whether the brand commands a price premium | No premium = brand is recognized but not valued |
| **Cornered Resource** | Unique data assets not available to competitors (count and depth) | Whether proprietary data advantage holds | Competitor announcing equivalent dataset = resource no longer cornered |
| **Cornered Resource** | Key talent retention rate (algorithm, data science teams) | Whether human cornered resources stay | Losing >20% of core team in a year = process power at risk |
| **Process Power** | Outcome accuracy vs. industry benchmark | Whether proprietary processes produce measurably better results | Competitors matching your outcomes = process power eroding |
| **Process Power** | Time for a new hire to reach full productivity on core processes | Whether process knowledge is codified vs. tribal | Long ramp time = knowledge is deep (good for defensibility); but also fragile (single points of failure) |

**How to use this table:** Pick the 4-6 KPIs most relevant to your product's power profile. Track them quarterly. If 2+ powers show declining KPIs in the same quarter, that's a strategic alert — your advantage is compressing and you need to invest or pivot.
