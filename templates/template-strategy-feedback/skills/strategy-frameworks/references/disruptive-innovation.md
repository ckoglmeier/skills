# Disruptive Innovation: Clayton Christensen's Framework

## Core Question
**Are we disrupting from below, or sustaining from above? Who gets disrupted, and when?**

Disruptive Innovation explains why incumbent companies with superior products often lose to inferior startups. The insight: sometimes "worse" products that are cheaper and simpler end up displacing "better" products. This framework helps you understand whether you're the disruptor or the disrupted.

---

## Two Types of Innovation

### Type 1: Sustaining Innovation (Incumbent Advantage)
**Definition:** Making existing products better for existing customers.

**Characteristics:**
- Better performance, more features, higher quality
- More expensive
- Serves current customers' evolving needs
- Incumbents are usually good at this

**Examples:**
- Smartphone cameras improving from 5MP to 200MP
- Larger hard drives (1TB → 2TB → 4TB)
- Faster processors
- Traditional recruiting: better candidate screening tools

**Who wins:** Incumbents usually win at sustaining innovation. They have resources, customer relationships, and incentive to invest.

### Type 2: Disruptive Innovation
**Definition:** Creating simpler, cheaper, "worse" products that eventually displace market leaders.

**The paradox:** Disruptive products are inferior on the dimensions that current customers care about. But they're superior on new dimensions (simplicity, cost, accessibility) that create new markets.

**Characteristics:**
- Worse performance on traditional metrics
- Much cheaper
- Simpler, easier to use
- Initially serves new market or non-consumers
- Incumbents ignore or dismiss them (and rationally so)

**Examples:**
- Discs vs. Vinyl: Discs were "worse" at sound quality but better at convenience
- Digital photography vs. film: Worse quality initially but better at accessibility
- Streaming vs. Cable TV: Worse channel selection initially but better at flexibility and cost
- Online universities vs. traditional colleges: Worse prestige initially but better at access and affordability

**Asymmetry of motivation:** Why don't incumbents copy the disruptor?
- Cannibalizes their existing business (streaming hurts cable TV revenue)
- Lower margin product (not profitable for their cost structure)
- Conflicts with customer relationships (Blockbuster can't undercut itself)
- Doesn't fit existing processes (ATS can't be as transparent without destroying lock-in)

## Why Incumbents Usually Lose to Disruption

Christensen's insight: Incumbents' rational behavior makes them vulnerable to disruption.

### Mechanism 1: Upmarket Migration
Incumbents naturally migrate upmarket (higher-margin customers, more demanding). This leaves low-end exposed to disruption.

**Example:** ATS vendors chased enterprise features (customization, integrations), leaving mid-market and SMB underserved. Disruptors took that segment.

**Risk:** If you stay focused on your differentiation and don't add complex features, you're potentially disruptive. But if you eventually need enterprise features, you might lose your differentiation.

### Mechanism 2: Asymmetry of Motivation
Disruptors are motivated to improve. Incumbents are motivated to maintain margins.

**Example:** Netflix (streaming) was motivated to grow streaming revenue. Cable companies were motivated to maintain high cable margins. When streaming improved, Netflix scaled aggressively; cable companies moved slower.

**Company dynamic:** You're motivated to prove your positioning works. Incumbents are motivated to maintain their existing roadmap. If your dimension becomes critical, you win. If existing factors stay critical, incumbents win.

### Mechanism 3: The Trap of Following Customers
Incumbents rationally follow their customers' lead. But by the time customers want the new thing, it's too late.

**Classic example:** Incumbent invents new technology but doesn't commercialize it because it cannibalizes existing margins.

**Company dynamic:** Staying focused on your differentiation while not adding features that dilute the new positioning.

## Common Mistakes with Disruptive Innovation Framework

### Mistake 1: Assuming disruptive products are always cheaper
Some disruptive innovations are cheap (smartphones disrupted feature phones). Some are expensive (Tesla disrupted gas cars on prestige/tech, not cost). Don't assume cheaper = disruptive.

### Mistake 2: Thinking the disrupted company is stupid
Incumbents aren't stupid; they're rational. They don't adopt disruptive strategies because it cannibalizes their margins. Eventually they're forced to, but by then it's too late.

### Mistake 3: Assuming disruption is inevitable
Many would-be disruptors never actually displace incumbents. They coexist in different segments forever. Disruption is possible, not guaranteed.

### Mistake 4: Confusing different strategies
- Blue Ocean (new positioning) ≠ Disruptive (simpler/cheaper, new market)
- Your company might be pursuing either or both strategies
- Be clear about which disruption model applies to your business

### Mistake 5: Failing to notice when you become the incumbent
If your company becomes successful and grows upmarket, you might stop being disruptive and start defending your turf. Watch for this trap.

## Strategic Implications

**If being disruptive:**
1. **Stay focused on your core positioning** — Don't dilute your differentiation
2. **Don't get pulled upmarket too fast** — Resist adding features that compromise your wedge
3. **Improve relentlessly** — Keep innovating along your differentiation axis
4. **Eventually scale** — Once the market values what you're optimized for, you can grow

**If pursuing a Blue Ocean strategy instead:**
1. **Create new market category** — Own the positioning you've defined
2. **Be the leader** — Establish yourself as the definitive player
3. **Integrate or coexist** — Decide whether you complement or replace incumbents
4. **Convince the market** — Demonstrate that your value proposition wins

**Key strategic question:** What does the market ultimately care more about? Your answer determines your strategy.

## Output Format: Disruptive Innovation Analysis

```
INCUMBENT DEFINITION:
- Who's being disrupted? [Traditional ATS vendors, recruiters, etc.]
- What's their value proposition? [Features, speed, efficiency?]
- Who are their customers? [Enterprises, mid-market?]

YOUR POSITIONING:
- Is it simpler? [Yes / No / Partially]
- Is it cheaper? [Yes / No / Partially]
- Does it serve non-consumers? [Yes / No / Partially]
- Does it serve overserved segments? [Yes / No / Partially]

DISRUPTIVE POTENTIAL:
- On a scale of 1-10, how disruptive is your approach? [1 = no disruption, 10 = classic disruptor]
- What's your disruptive wedge? [If any]
- At what scale does disruption become real?

INCUMBENT RESPONSE:
- Are incumbents ignoring you? [Yes / Somewhat / Already competing]
- Why? [Market too small, different positioning, margin loss, etc.]
- When will they take you seriously? [What milestone triggers response?]

ASYMMETRY OF MOTIVATION:
- Are you more motivated to improve along your dimension than incumbents? [Yes / No / Unclear]
- Is there a conflict between your dimension and incumbents' business model? [Yes / No / Unclear]

VULNERABILITY TIMELINE:
- Can traditional approaches coexist long-term? [Yes / No / Uncertain]
- At what point does your dimension become existential for incumbents?

STRATEGIC IMPLICATION:
- Should you play the disruptive game or Blue Ocean game? [Disrupt / Create new category / Coexist]
- What needs to be true for your strategy to succeed?
```
