# Jobs to Be Done: Clayton Christensen's Framework

## Core Question
**What job is the customer hiring this product to do? (And is it the job you think?)**

Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) is about understanding the **real problem** customers are trying to solve, not the features you're selling them. The critical insight: customers don't want your product; they want to solve a problem. Understand the problem, not the product.

The classic example: Customers don't buy drills. They buy the ability to make holes in walls.

---

## The Three Jobs

Every purchase involves three jobs:

### 1. The Functional Job
**The practical task the customer is trying to accomplish.**

Examples:
- "I need to hire a developer in the next 2 weeks"
- "I need to know which bootcamp to attend to get a job"
- "I need to place graduates into jobs quickly"

**How to identify:**
- What problem does the customer face?
- What do they currently do to solve it? (Status quo)
- What's the desired state?

### 2. The Emotional Job
**The feeling or identity the customer wants to achieve.**

Examples:
- "I want to feel confident that I'm hiring fairly and transparently"
- "I want to feel like I made a smart career investment"
- "I want to feel like I'm helping my learners succeed"

**How to identify:**
- How do they want to *feel*?
- What fears or anxieties are they solving for?
- What does success *feel* like to them?

### 3. The Social Job
**How the customer wants to be perceived by others.**

Examples:
- "I want to be seen as a fair, ethical employer"
- "I want to be seen as a learner who's serious about my career"
- "I want to be seen as an institution that produces successful graduates"

**How to identify:**
- How do they want others to perceive them?
- What reputation do they want?
- Who's their reference group?

## The Critical Insight: Different Customers, Different Jobs

**The Milkshake Problem (Christensen's classic example):**

McDonald's wanted to improve milkshake sales. They surveyed customers: "What can we improve?" (taste, texture, price, etc.). They made changes. Sales didn't improve.

Then they asked a different question: "What job are you hiring the milkshake to do?"

Answer: Customers on their morning commute were hiring milkshakes to make their boring drive entertaining and keep them full until lunch. The job wasn't "taste good." The job was "be a vehicle for an entertaining, filling commute experience."

**McDonald's insight:** Different customer segments hired the milkshake for different jobs. A business customer on a commute had a different job than a teenager at a movie theater. Same product, different jobs.

**Implication:** You can't understand customer needs without asking about the job.

## Company Application: What Jobs Are Customers Hiring You to Do?

When company context is configured, apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

### Key Questions for Each Customer Segment:

**Functional jobs:**
- What practical task are they trying to accomplish?
- What's their desired outcome?

**Emotional jobs:**
- How do they want to feel?
- What anxieties are they solving for?

**Social jobs:**
- How do they want to be perceived?
- By whom do they want to be seen this way?

**Variant jobs by segment:**
- How do different customer segments within each group have different jobs?
- What's shared across segments? What's unique?

**Risk assessment:**
- Are we addressing all three job types, or just the functional?
- Will customers churn if we only solve the functional job?

## How to Discover the Real Jobs

### Method 1: The Outcome-Focused Interview
Don't ask about the product. Ask about the change they want:
- "What brought you to our product?"
- "What were you trying to accomplish?"
- "What wasn't working before?"
- "How would you know if this product succeeded?"
- "What would you tell others about why you use this product?"

**Listen for:** The emotional and social dimensions in their answers, not just the functional.

### Method 2: The Context and Circumstance
Jobs emerge from context. Understand when/where/how they use your product:
- "Walk me through the last time you used this product. Where were you? What were you trying to do?"
- "What was happening right before you decided to use this product?"
- "What else were you doing at the same time?"

**Listen for:** What problem just emerged? What anxiety were they experiencing?

### Method 3: The Constraint and Trade-off
Jobs are revealed by trade-offs:
- "What did you give up to use this instead of [alternative]?"
- "What would have to be true for you to leave?"
- "If this product lost [feature], would you still use it? Why?"

**Listen for:** What really matters? What would they accept losing?

### Method 4: Longitudinal / Jobs Evolving
Jobs change as customers grow and context changes:
- "How has your use of this product changed over time?"
- "Is it serving a different purpose now than when you started?"
- "What's changed about your situation?"

**Listen for:** Which jobs are durable? Which evolve with context?

## Output: Job Statements

Once you understand the jobs, articulate them clearly:

**Job statement template:**
"When [customer type] is [situation/context], they hire [your product] to **[functional job]** AND **[emotional job]** AND **[social job]**."

This forces you to articulate all three dimensions. If you can't fill all three, you're missing something about how the job works.

---

## Common Mistakes with JTBD

### Mistake 1: Confusing customer problems with jobs
A problem is "I don't have enough candidates." A job is "I need to feel confident that I can hire quality people without wasting time." The job is deeper.

### Mistake 2: Assuming one job per customer type
The same customer might hire your product for different jobs in different contexts. Understand how each customer segment uses you differently.

### Mistake 3: Only addressing the functional job
Functional jobs are table stakes. Emotional and social jobs are what create loyalty and defensibility. If your competitor matches you on features, they lose on emotional resonance (or vice versa).

### Mistake 4: Assuming your job hypothesis is right without research
You think learners care about "finding jobs"; they actually care about "validating my education investment." These lead to very different product priorities.

### Mistake 5: Forgetting that jobs evolve
A learner's job on day 1 (get hired) is different from day 30 (succeed in my role). A bootcamp's job at launch (place people) is different from year 3 (be known for quality outcomes). Products that only serve the initial job fail when jobs evolve.

---

## Strategic Implications

**If the jobs are correctly understood:**
- Product roadmap should address all three (functional, emotional, social)
- Marketing should speak to emotional and social jobs, not just functional
- Success metrics should include emotional satisfaction, not just volume
- Retention will be tied to how well you serve the emotional and social jobs

**If the jobs aren't understood:**
- You'll optimize for the wrong metrics (placements instead of confidence)
- You'll lose to competitors that understand the emotional/social jobs better
- You'll churn because you're solving a problem, not addressing a need
- You'll struggle to build a defensible competitive advantage (anyone can optimize the functional job)

## Output Format: Jobs to Be Done Analysis

```
PRIMARY CUSTOMER SEGMENT: [Learners / Employers / Institutions]

FUNCTIONAL JOB:
- Core problem: [What are they trying to accomplish?]
- Current solution: [How do they do it today?]
- Frustration points: [Where does the current solution fail?]
- Desired outcome: [What would success look like?]

EMOTIONAL JOB:
- Underlying anxiety/fear: [What are they worried about?]
- Desired feeling: [How should they feel if this succeeds?]
- Evidence of success: [What signals would they notice?]

SOCIAL JOB:
- Desired perception: [How do they want to be seen?]
- Reference group: [By whom do they want to be seen this way?]
- Status/credibility signal: [What proves they've achieved this job?]

OVERALL JOB STATEMENT:
"When [context], [customer] hires [your product] to [functional job] AND [emotional job] AND [social job]."

VARIANT JOBS BY SEGMENT:
[Repeat for each major customer segment]

STRATEGIC IMPLICATION:
- Which job are we currently optimizing for? [Functional only / Functional + emotional / All three]
- Which job are we missing? [What's underserved?]
- How should this change our product/marketing/metrics?
```
