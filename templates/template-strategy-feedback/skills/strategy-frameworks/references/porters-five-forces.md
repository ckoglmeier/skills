# Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure Analysis

## Core Question
**What's the structural power dynamic in our industry, and who wins?**

Porter's Five Forces analyzes the competitive structure of an industry. Rather than looking at individual competitors, it looks at the *structural forces* that determine profitability and power. These forces shape whether competition is intense or mellow, whether margins compress or stay healthy, and who has leverage.

## The 5 Forces

### 1. Supplier Power
**Definition:** How much power do your suppliers have to raise prices, reduce quality, or impose conditions on you?

**What drives supplier power UP (bad for you):**
- Few suppliers, many customers (suppliers can be picky)
- Suppliers are larger or more concentrated than customers
- Switching costs are high (you're locked into your suppliers)
- Suppliers' products are differentiated (you can't easily find substitutes)
- Suppliers can vertically integrate forward and compete with you

**What drives supplier power DOWN (good for you):**
- Many suppliers, few customers (you have leverage)
- You can switch easily to alternatives
- Suppliers are fragmented and compete on price
- Your volume is material to the supplier (you're a valuable customer)

**How to assess it:**
1. List your key suppliers (content creators, technology vendors, fulfillment partners, etc.)
2. For each: How concentrated are they? (1-2 suppliers = high power; 10+ = low power)
3. How much would it cost you to switch? (High cost = their power increases)
4. Can they forward-integrate? (If a content vendor could become an employer platform, they have leverage)
5. What % of their business are you? (If you're <5%, they have power; if >25%, you have power)

**Company-Specific Application:**
When company context is configured, apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

**Common mistakes:**
- Focusing only on direct suppliers (forget about suppliers to your suppliers)
- Assuming brand-name vendors have high power (they usually don't in a competitive market)
- Confusing critical to your business with high supplier power

### 2. Buyer Power
**Definition:** How much power do your customers have to demand lower prices, better terms, or force conditions on you?

**What drives buyer power UP (bad for you):**
- Few large customers (their loss is material to you)
- Your product is undifferentiated (easy for them to switch)
- Low switching costs (they can leave easily)
- Buyers threaten to integrate backward (make your product themselves)
- Buyers are price-sensitive and price-conscious

**What drives buyer power DOWN (good for you):**
- Many small customers (no single customer is critical)
- You're differentiated (they can't easily find substitutes)
- High switching costs (they're locked in)
- Your product is high-value to them (low price sensitivity)
- Fragmented buyer base (no coordination among buyers)

**How to assess it:**
1. Who are your top customers? What % of revenue do they represent? (Concentration = their power)
2. How easy is it for them to switch to a competitor? (Switching cost?)
3. Is your product critical to their business or nice-to-have? (Criticality = your power)
4. Could they build this themselves? (Backward integration threat?)
5. How price-sensitive are they? (Are they shopping on price, or value?)

**Company-Specific Application:**
When company context is configured, apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

**Common mistakes:**
- Overestimating how easy it is for customers to switch (they usually won't, despite complaints)
- Underestimating large customer power (one customer representing 30% of revenue has enormous leverage)
- Confusing customer complaints with buyer power (complaints don't equal switching behavior)

### 3. Threat of Substitutes
**Definition:** How easily can your customers solve their problem a different way (not through a direct competitor, but through a substitute)?

**What it looks like:**
- Alternative solutions to the same problem (different technology, business model, or approach)
- Not direct competitors, but functional alternatives
- Growing attractiveness or accessibility of the substitute

**Examples:**
- For a staffing platform: direct hiring, outsourced recruiters, universities, coding bootcamps
- For a productivity app: spreadsheets, pen-and-paper, building custom tools
- For consulting: doing it in-house, AI/automation, training your own team

**How to assess it:**
1. What is the customer really trying to solve? (The "job to be done")
2. What are ALL the ways they could solve it? (Don't limit to direct competitors)
3. How attractive are substitutes? (Cost, effort, results, time to value)
4. Are substitutes improving faster than your solution? (Technology curves matter)
5. What % of customers use substitutes? (And is that % growing?)

**Company-Specific Application:**
When company context is configured, apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

**Common mistakes:**
- Only counting direct competitors as threats (substitutes are usually more dangerous)
- Assuming the status quo is a substitute (internal recruiting isn't "traditional" — it's what they already do, so it's actually a high threat)
- Underestimating how attractive existing solutions are to customers (they're sticky)

### 4. Threat of New Entrants
**Definition:** How easily can new competitors enter your market and take share?

**Barriers to entry determine threat level:**

**High barriers (good for incumbents):**
- Scale economies (new entrant must reach minimum efficient scale before profitability)
- Brand/switching costs (incumbents have lock-in)
- Capital requirements (expensive to build)
- Regulatory requirements (licenses, certifications)
- Access to distribution (hard to reach customers)
- Proprietary technology or data

**Low barriers (bad for incumbents):**
- No economies of scale (SMB can compete on equal footing)
- Low brand loyalty (customers easily switch)
- Low capital requirements (easy to launch)
- No regulatory moats
- Easy distribution (digital, marketplace, etc.)

**How to assess it:**
1. What barriers protect us from new entrants? (List each, assess strength)
2. What would a new entrant need to compete? (Capital, time, talent, data)
3. Could a venture-backed startup beat us? (If yes, barriers are low)
4. Are barriers strengthening (good) or eroding (bad)? (Trends matter)
5. Are there emerging adjacencies from where competitors could enter? (Adjacent players are dangerous)

**Company-Specific Application:**
When company context is configured, apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

**Common mistakes:**
- Overestimating the difficulty of entry (thinking technology or capital is a bigger barrier than it is)
- Assuming brand is enough (it's not; customers will switch if the alternative is materially better)
- Underestimating how fast ventures can move (VCs fund speed; you can't outrun a funded competitor by moving slowly)

### 5. Competitive Rivalry
**Definition:** How intense is competition among existing competitors in your market?

**What drives rivalry UP (bad for you):**
- Many competitors of roughly equal strength
- Slow industry growth (fighting for share, not growing together)
- High fixed costs (competitors desperate to fill capacity)
- Low differentiation (competing on price)
- Switching costs are low (customers easily jump between you)
- High exit barriers (competitors stuck competing because leaving is expensive)
- Diverse strategies (competitors pursuing different approaches, creating instability)

**What drives rivalry DOWN (good for you):**
- Few competitors or clear leader (in a fragmented market)
- High growth (expanding pie, not zero-sum)
- High differentiation (competing on unique factors)
- Switching costs are high (customers locked in)
- Clear competitive hierarchy (everyone knows who wins, accepts it)
- Low exit barriers (weak competitors leave, consolidation happens)

**How to assess it:**
1. Who are the direct competitors? (Map them)
2. How similar are your offerings? (Differentiation or homogeneity?)
3. Is the market growing or shrinking? (Growth = less rivalry)
4. What are competitors' strategic objectives? (Maximize profit vs. gain share? Different motives = different intensity)
5. Are exit barriers high or low? (Determines who stays and fights)

**Company-Specific Application:**
When company context is configured, apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

**Common mistakes:**
- Overweighting the threat of existing competitors (new entrants and substitutes are often more dangerous)
- Assuming rivalry is permanent at current intensity (changes with market growth, consolidation, entry/exit)
- Underestimating how fast a new entrant can disrupt competitive dynamics

## Five Forces Analysis Template

For a complete analysis, answer each question systematically:

```
SUPPLIER POWER: [High / Moderate / Low]
Key suppliers: [List]
Power drivers: [Concentration? Differentiation? Switching costs? Vertical integration threat?]
Company impact: [How does this shape our strategy? Where are vulnerabilities?]

BUYER POWER: [High / Moderate / Low]
Key customers: [Concentration of revenue by customer]
Power drivers: [Switching costs? Differentiation? Backward integration threat? Price sensitivity?]
Company impact: [How do we maintain pricing power? Which segments have leverage?]

THREAT OF SUBSTITUTES: [High / Moderate / Low]
Key substitutes: [List]
Substitute attractiveness: [Cost? Effectiveness? Ease? Trend?]
Company impact: [How do we compete against substitutes? Where are we vulnerable?]

THREAT OF NEW ENTRANTS: [High / Moderate / Low]
Barriers to entry: [List each barrier and assess strength]
Likely entrants: [Venture competitors? Adjacent players? Corporate ventures?]
Company impact: [How do we defend against new entrants?]

COMPETITIVE RIVALRY: [High / Moderate / Low]
Number and strength of competitors: [Fragmented or concentrated?]
Rivalry drivers: [Growth rate? Differentiation? Exit barriers?]
Company impact: [How do we compete? Where do we have advantage?]

OVERALL ASSESSMENT:
- Most attractive force (lowest threat): [Which force? Why?]
- Most threatening force: [Which force? Why? What to do about it?]
- Strategic implications: [What does this structure mean for company strategy?]
```

---

## Output Format: Porter's Five Forces Brief

Present findings as:

1. **Competitive Landscape Summary** — A 2-3 sentence description of industry structure and competitive intensity

2. **Force-by-force assessment** — For each force, state the level (H/M/L) and 2-3 key drivers

3. **Power and profitability implications** — Who has leverage? Where do margins compress? How does this shape pricing power?

4. **Strategic recommendations** — What should the company do given this structure? (E.g., if buyer power is high, deepen switching costs)
