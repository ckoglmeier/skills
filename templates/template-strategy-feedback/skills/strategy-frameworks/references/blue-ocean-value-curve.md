# Blue Ocean / Value Curve: Strategic Positioning

## Core Question
**What do we compete on vs. refuse to compete on? Where's the blue ocean?**

Blue Ocean Strategy (Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne) flips traditional competition strategy. Instead of fighting competitors on their turf (Red Ocean), you create a new market where competition is irrelevant (Blue Ocean).

The tool is the **Value Curve** — a visual that shows where you compete, where you don't, where you lead, and where you differ from competitors.

---

## Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean

**Red Ocean (Traditional Competition):**
- Compete on known factors (everyone agrees on what matters)
- Win by doing it better/cheaper than competitors
- Zero-sum: your gain is competitor's loss
- Market eventually commoditizes (race to the bottom)
- Example: Southwest vs. legacy airlines — shorter flights, cheaper, faster turnaround (but both competing on cost/convenience)

**Blue Ocean (New Market Creation):**
- Create new factors that competitors don't (redefine what matters)
- Win by making competition irrelevant (you're in a different game)
- Non-zero-sum: grow the market, don't fight for share
- Market expands, margins stay healthy
- Example: Netflix vs. Blockbuster — not just "store with more movies" but "watch at home whenever you want" (different game entirely)

## The Value Curve: Four Actions

The value curve identifies four things:

### 1. Factors to Eliminate
**What does the industry compete on that we should NOT?**

Examples:
- Netflix eliminated: Late fees, specific movie availability in stores, membership negotiations, store locations
- Example: Eliminate bulk volume focus, replace with quality focus

**How to identify:**
- What factors do competitors obsess over?
- Which factors do customers actually NOT value?
- Which factors are expensive for you to maintain?
- What would customers accept us dropping if we excelled elsewhere?

**Risk:** Eliminating factors that customers actually care about is suicide. Only eliminate factors that:
- Cost you money to maintain
- Customers don't actually value
- Prevent you from investing in higher-value factors

### 2. Factors to Reduce
**What factors do we compete on less than the industry average?**

Examples:
- Netflix reduced: Cost to entry (basic plan very cheap), movie selection (not as broad as cable), customer service (self-service)
- Example: Reduce volume focus, start with quality partners only

**How to identify:**
- Where do we intentionally under-invest?
- Where do we offer less than industry standard?
- Why? What do we gain by not winning here?

**Strategy:** Reduce costs where customers don't need maximum. Invest those savings in Raise and Create.

### 3. Factors to Raise
**Where do we compete HARDER than the industry average?**

Examples:
- Netflix raised: Recommendation accuracy, convenience (no leaving house), personalization, catalog depth (once streaming infrastructure existed)
- Example: Raise match quality, transparency, outcomes data

**How to identify:**
- Where do we invest disproportionately?
- Where do we dramatically outperform competitors?
- What do customers notice and love?
- What creates defensibility?

**Strategy:** Make these factors the reason customers choose you.

### 4. Factors to Create
**What new factors does the industry NOT compete on that we should?**

Examples:
- Netflix created: Streaming (vs. renting DVDs), original content, algorithm-driven curation, mobile watching
- Example: Create long-term outcome tracking, transparent metrics

**How to identify:**
- What do customers secretly want but don't have?
- What would unlock new customer segments?
- What would make competition irrelevant?
- What can we do that competitors can't/won't?

**Risk:** Creating factors that nobody wants is expensive and pointless. Only create factors that:
- Customers will pay for (new willingness to pay)
- Competitors can't easily copy (or won't, due to business model conflict)
- Unlock new market (expand TAM)

## Strategic Implications

### If Your Blue Ocean is Valid:
1. **Compete on a different axis** — Create non-zero-sum competition
2. **Attract new customer segments** — Customers who value what you're creating
3. **Expand TAM** — Create demand among underserved customers
4. **Build defensibility** — Hard-to-replicate factors that competitors can't easily match
5. **Escape commoditization** — Different game entirely

### Risks to Your Value Curve:
1. **Eliminate factors customers actually need** — Your curve fails if you remove something critical
2. **Create factors nobody will pay for** — Sound good but don't drive behavior change
3. **Raise factors competitors can easily copy** — Your defensibility disappears
4. **Misread customer priorities** — You're optimizing the wrong factors

## How to Validate Your Value Curve

### 1. Interview test
Ask target customers (employers, institutions, learners):
- "What's most important to you in a hiring solution?" (Get priorities)
- "What do you wish hiring solutions offered?" (Unmet needs)
- "Would fairness metrics change how you hire?" (Create factor value)
- "Would you accept slower hiring for better matches?" (Raise factor trade-off)
- "Would you pay more for transparency?" (Price test)

### 2. Cohort test
A/B test your value curve with different customer segments:
- Segment 1: Traditional players (care about speed/volume) — Probably don't want your curve
- Segment 2: Value-aligned segment (care about your differentiators) — Probably love your curve
- Segment 3: Outcome-focused segment (care about results/reputation) — Probably love your curve

If one segment strongly prefers your curve, you've found the blue ocean.

### 3. Competitor response test
If you're on a true Blue Ocean, competitors either:
- Ignore you (your market is too niche)
- Try to copy but can't (your curve requires capabilities they lack)
- Copy but dilute their own advantage (they can't maintain their curve + yours)

If competitors easily adopt your curve, you haven't created a blue ocean — you've just created a new competitive factor.

## Common Mistakes with Value Curves

### Mistake 1: "Blue Ocean" that's too small
Eliminating and reducing factors that most customers care about means you've appealed to a niche. That's fine, but it's not a Blue Ocean — it's Red Ocean for a small segment.

**Test:** Is your addressable market large enough? (If you've eliminated factors that 80% of market needs, you're not expanding TAM — you're shrinking it.)

### Mistake 2: Creating factors that sound good but don't drive behavior
You create "employer scorecards" and think it's innovative. But employers don't actually use them or change behavior because of them. Empty create factor.

**Test:** Do customers actually use/value the factors you create? Or do they politely ignore them?

### Mistake 3: Raising factors that aren't actually raising (just same)
You think you're raising "match quality" but you're not materially different than competitors. You've raised nothing — you're just claiming to be "better."

**Test:** Can you quantify the raise? (E.g., "Match quality: traditional competitors 45% 1-year retention, us 65%")

### Mistake 4: Reducing so much that you lose critical functionality
You reduce "bulk hiring" and "feature customization," but it turns out large employers need both. You've eliminated your own market.

**Test:** Do customers churn when they realize you've eliminated something they need?

### Mistake 5: Value curve that doesn't match your actual product
You say you've created "long-term success tracking," but you don't actually have 1-year outcome data yet. You've got aspirational positioning, not a real value curve.

**Test:** Can you actually deliver on every factor in your curve today? If not, when? (Roadmap vs. positioning)
