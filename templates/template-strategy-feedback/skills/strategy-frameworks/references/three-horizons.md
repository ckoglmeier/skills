# Three Horizons: McKinsey's Resource Allocation Framework

## Core Question
**How should we allocate resources across core business, emerging growth, and future bets?**

The Three Horizons Framework addresses the fundamental tension in strategy: How do you defend today's business while building tomorrow's? Most companies starve the future to feed the present. This framework forces explicit choice about resource allocation.

---

## The Three Horizons

### Horizon 1: Core Business (Defend & Extend)
**Focus:** Maximize cash flow and profitability from existing business

**Characteristics:**
- Current revenue driver (generates most cash today)
- Known customers, known markets
- Mature, but still improving
- Defend against competition; extend into adjacent segments

**Examples:**
- Current core product/service generating revenue
- Primary customer segments
- Core capabilities being refined

**Resource allocation:** 60-70% of resources typically go here

**Key metrics:**
- Revenue growth
- Margin expansion
- Customer retention
- Market share defense

**Strategic questions:**
- Where are we being threatened? (Competitive response needed?)
- Can we extend into adjacent segments with existing offering? (Pricing expansion, adjacent use cases?)
- Are we optimizing margin or growth? (Different resource allocation based on answer)

### Horizon 2: Emerging Business (Build & Scale)
**Focus:** Build new growth engines that will drive future revenue

**Characteristics:**
- Future revenue driver (generates significant revenue in 3-5 years)
- New markets, new customer segments, or new business models
- Still developing, not yet profitable
- Require investment; can't be self-sustaining yet

**Examples:**
- New customer segments not yet served
- Adjacent product extensions
- New business models or revenue streams
- Geographic expansion

**Resource allocation:** 20-30% of resources typically go here

**Key metrics:**
- Growth rate (faster than H1)
- Time to profitability
- Market validation (customer acquisition rate)
- Competitive positioning

**Strategic questions:**
- Which emerging market will be most valuable in 5 years?
- How much investment does each need to reach scale?
- When will each reach profitability?
- How do we resource these without starving H1?

### Horizon 3: Exploratory/Options (Seed & Option)
**Focus:** Explore possible futures; place bets on transformative opportunities

**Characteristics:**
- Speculative (high uncertainty, high upside)
- Early stage (often not yet revenue-generating)
- High-risk, high-reward
- Options on the future (you own the right to scale later)

**Examples:**
- Emerging technologies relevant to your space
- New market categories or ecosystems
- Transformative business model innovations
- Expansion into adjacent industries or use cases

**Resource allocation:** 5-10% of resources typically go here

**Key metrics:**
- Learning rate (How fast are we validating hypotheses?)
- Option value (What's the upside if this works?)
- Time to decision point (When do we know if this scales?)

**Strategic questions:**
- What future capability/market would be transformative?
- What small bets can we place to own that future?
- Which bets should we kill vs. continue?
- How do we know when a H3 bet should graduate to H2?

---

## The Tension Between Horizons

The fundamental dynamic:

**H1 demands resources:** Core business has immediate revenue. Investors want growth. Customers need support. It's impossible to say "no" to H1 demands. Result: H1 gets 80-90% of resources, leaving H2 and H3 starved.

**H2 needs resources:** Future growth requires investment NOW. But H2 isn't yet profitable, so it's easy to defer. Classic mistake: By the time you start H2, it's too late to capture the emerging market.

**H3 seems optional:** Exploration is nice-to-have. First sign of trouble, H3 gets cut. But H3 is how you option the future.

**Common outcome:** Company is 100% focused on H1, loses emerging market to competitor, and years later, tries to catch up (too late).

## Resource Allocation by Life Stage

### Early-stage company:
**Typical allocation:**
- H1: 70% (build and prove core offering)
- H2: 25% (explore adjacent markets)
- H3: 5% (place bets on future)

**Rationale:** You need to prove the core works before building adjacent businesses.

### Growth-stage company:
**Typical allocation:**
- H1: 60% (defend and extend core)
- H2: 30% (scale emerging business)
- H3: 10% (seed future options)

**Rationale:** Core is proven; now scaling adjacent market is critical.

### Mature company (ATS vendors today):
**Typical allocation:**
- H1: 50% (defend core; margins matter)
- H2: 30% (build new businesses or new channels)
- H3: 20% (place bigger future bets; need transformation)

**Rationale:** Core is mature; growth comes from elsewhere.

## Company Horizon Questions

### Horizon 1: What's our core?
- What's the primary offering? (Primary revenue today?)
- What are related offerings at similar maturity?
- What's our primary customer segment?
- Can we grow within H1? (Upsell, adjacent segments, geographic expansion?)

### Horizon 2: What's our emerging growth engine?
- Direct-to-learner (learner acquisition without institutions)?
- Employer retention/success (expand beyond hiring)?
- Institutional outcomes funding (new revenue stream)?
- Geographic expansion?

**Resource question:** How much should we invest in H2 while H1 is still scaling?
- Too little: Miss the emerging market
- Too much: Starve H1 and risk core business

### Horizon 3: What could be transformative in 10 years?
- AI-powered career development (personalized paths)?
- Ecosystem play (become the infrastructure for hiring)?
- New verticals (healthcare hiring, manufacturing hiring)?
- International (different markets, different dynamics)?

**Resource question:** How many H3 bets should we have active?
- 3-5 small bets is typical (explore futures, kill losers, scale winners)

## Common Mistakes with Three Horizons

### Mistake 1: Ignoring H1
Some founders become obsessed with H2/H3 ("future opportunity") and starve H1. H1 is your cash engine; you must win there first. Fix: Allocate to H1 first, then H2 and H3 with remainder.

### Mistake 2: Not allocating enough to H2
Classic mistake: H1 grows, consumes all resources. H2 never gets funded. By the time you decide H2 is important, the window has passed. Fix: Explicit resource allocation; "ring-fence" H2 budget so it's not raided by H1 needs.

### Mistake 3: Confusing H2 and H3
- H2 is next growth engine (should be profitable in 3-5 years)
- H3 is speculative bet (might never be profitable)

If you're treating H2 as H3 (speculative), you're not investing enough. If you're treating H3 as H2 (expecting near-term ROI), you're killing future bets too early.

### Mistake 4: Not establishing different success metrics by horizon
- H1: Profitability, growth, margin, retention
- H2: Growth rate, time to profitability, market validation
- H3: Learning rate, option value, decision point timeline

If you judge H2 by H1 metrics, you'll kill it. If you judge H3 by H1 metrics, you'll kill it. Different horizons, different measures of success.

### Mistake 5: Not graduating bets from H3 to H2
Sometimes H3 bets validate and should become H2. If you don't have a graduation process, you either leave bets in H3 (missing growth opportunity) or suddenly shift all resources to H3 (starving H1 and H2).

## The H2/H3 Resource Conversation

**Executive question:** "We're resource-constrained. Which do we fund: H2 emerging business or H3 future option?"

**Answer:** It depends on:

**Fund H2 more if:**
- H1 is mature and stable (can sustain itself)
- H2 market is clear and large (obvious upside)
- You have leadership and product-market fit for H2
- Time-to-profitability is clear (not open-ended)

**Fund H3 more if:**
- H1 and H2 are strong (can sustain themselves)
- Future threats are existential (disruption risk)
- You want optionality (don't know which bet will win)
- You have people who love exploring (don't just make everyone optimize H1)

---

## Operational Questions

**On H1/H2 balance:**
1. How much does H1 need to remain stable?
2. How much would meaningfully move H2?
3. What's the competitive risk if we don't fund H2?
4. What's the risk-adjusted ROI for each H2 candidate?

**On H2 candidates:**
- Which bet has the best ROI/resource efficiency?
- What's the timeline to revenue for each?
- Which one can you realistically execute?
- Pick one; don't try to do them all.

**On H3:**
- How many H3 bets should you place? (3-5 is typical)
- Budget per bet?
- Decision point for each? (When do you know if it's viable?)
- Kill criteria? (What signals mean "stop"?)

- Kill criteria: [What signals mean we stop?]

RESOURCE ALLOCATION:
| Horizon | % of Resources | $ Budget | Key Metrics |
|---------|----------------|----------|-------------|
| H1 | [X]% | $[Y]M | [Growth, margin, retention] |
| H2 | [X]% | $[Y]M | [Growth rate, path to profit] |
| H3 | [X]% | $[Y]M | [Learning rate, option value] |

TENSIONS & DECISIONS:
- H1 is demanding resources. How do we protect H2/H3? [Explicit allocation? Separate team?]
