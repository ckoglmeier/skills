# SaaS-like Network vs. True Marketplace: Casey Winters' Framework

## Core Question
**What kind of platform are we really building? And does it match our go-to-market strategy?**

Casey Winters identified that many platforms think they're marketplaces but are actually SaaS-like networks. This confusion leads to wrong go-to-market, wrong unit economics, and wrong unit economics. Understanding which model you are is critical.

---

## Two Models Defined

### Model 1: SaaS-like Network (Supply-Driven Growth)
**Core dynamic:** Supply drives demand. Grow supply, demand follows.

**How it works:**
1. Start with strong supply (recruit a lot of sellers/creators/experts)
2. Supply creates network gravity (supply quality attracts buyers)
3. Buyers arrive because of supply quality, not because of demand generation
4. 75-100% of demand comes from supply growth

**Metrics:**
- Supply growth ≈ Demand growth
- Network effects are weak or indirect (more supply → better experiences → more demand, but not cross-side network effects)
- Unit economics driven by supply productivity (how much revenue does one supplier generate?)

**Examples:**
- Airbnb: Grew by recruiting hosts. Once hosts existed, guests appeared.
- Etsy: Grew by recruiting sellers. Once unique sellers existed, buyers came.
- YouTube: Grew by recruiting creators. Once good content existed, viewers came.
- Uber early: Grew by recruiting drivers. Once drivers existed, riders appeared.

**Why it works:**
Supply creates proof-of-concept for demand. Buyers don't need to be convinced; the quality of supply does the convincing.

### Model 2: True Marketplace (Demand-Driven Growth)
**Core dynamic:** Demand drives supply. Attract and retain demand, supply follows.

**How it works:**
1. Start with strong demand (attract many buyers)
2. Strong demand attracts supply (suppliers want access to large buyer base)
3. Supply and demand reinforce each other (cross-side network effects)
4. 50-75% of growth from demand generation (active marketing, outbound, etc.)

**Metrics:**
- Demand growth ≠ Supply growth; demand growth is primary
- Strong cross-side network effects (more buyers → attracts suppliers → attracts more buyers)
- Unit economics driven by buyer acquisition cost and lifetime value

**Examples:**
- Uber now: Marketed to riders; drivers followed.
- DoorDash: Attracted customers with acquisition spending; restaurants followed.
- Stripe: Attracted merchants (demand) who wanted payment processing; demand attracted suppliers.
- Instacart: Attracted customers; stores followed.

**Why it works:**
Demand is expensive but creates proof-of-concept for supply. Suppliers don't want to join a platform with no users.

---

## The 50% Rule for Network Effects

Casey Winters identified a diagnostic: **In true marketplaces, you need approximately 50% of growth coming from one side (usually demand) and 50% from the other side.**

**Why?**
- If supply growth is 75%+, you're a SaaS-like network (supply-driven)
- If demand growth is 75%+, you're a demand-driven marketplace (most are demand-driven early)
- If it's 50/50, you have true cross-side network effects

**Company-Specific Application:**
When company context is configured, apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

**Key diagnostic questions:**
- What % of new users come from supply-side growth vs. demand-side growth?
- Are there cross-side network effects (demand attracts supply, supply attracts demand)?
- Or does one side drive all growth?

## Implications for Your Company

### If You're SaaS-like Network (Supply-Driven):
**What this means:**
- Prioritize supply recruitment
- Growth marketing for demand is secondary
- Network effects are weaker
- Growth depends on constant supply recruitment

**Go-to-market implications:**
- Invest in supply partnerships and recruitment
- Reach demand through supply
- Build defensibility through supply exclusivity

**Strengths:**
- Clearer unit economics
- Supply partnerships are defensible
- Can move fast early

**Risks:**
- Competitors can cherry-pick your best supply
- Supply can work with competitors too
- No self-sustaining flywheel

### If You're a True Marketplace (Demand-Driven):
**What this means:**
- Prioritize demand acquisition
- Supply follows demand
- Strong cross-side network effects
- Flywheel is self-sustaining

**Go-to-market implications:**
- Invest in demand marketing/acquisition
- Supply will naturally follow
- Build defensibility through network effects

**Strengths:**
- Self-sustaining growth
- Hard to compete against
- Larger TAM potential

**Risks:**
- Demand acquisition is expensive
- Chicken-egg problem early
- Vulnerable to competitors with bigger budgets

## The Critical Risk: Mistaking Your Model

**The trap:**
You think you're one model but you're actually the other. This leads to:
- Investing in the wrong growth lever
- Building the wrong type of defensibility
- Wasting resources on ineffective channels

**Test:** Pull your growth attribution data. If supply-side growth is >70%, you're probably SaaS-like. If demand-side is >70%, you're probably marketplace.

---

## Diagnostics: Which Model Are You?

### Test 1: Where does growth come from?
**Question:** In the past 3 months, what % of new users came from:
- Supply-side growth (new institutions → new learners)
- Demand-side growth (marketing, job boards, word-of-mouth)

**If >70% supply-side:** You're SaaS-like network
**If 40-60% split:** You might be either
**If >70% demand-side:** You're demand-driven marketplace

### Test 2: Can you grow without the other side?
**Question:** If institutions disappeared, could you still grow with learners + employers? Or if employers disappeared, could you still grow with learners + institutions?

**If no:** You're SaaS-like (supply is critical)
**If yes:** You're marketplace (both sides are independently valuable)

### Test 3: Are there cross-side network effects?
**Question:** Do customers choose your platform because of supply density? Does supply grow because of demand density?

**If weak:** You're SaaS-like (supply drives demand, but not the other way)
**If strong:** You're marketplace (both sides reinforce each other)

### Test 4: What breaks first when you stop recruiting?
**Question:** If you stop recruiting institutions, do learners churn? If you stop marketing to learners, do employers churn?

**If learners churn without institutions:** SaaS-like
**If nothing breaks:** Marketplace (both sides are self-sustaining)

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Building a SaaS-like network but executing marketplace go-to-market
You recruit institutions (supply), but then spend money on learner acquisition (demand). That's backwards. Institutions drive learners; you should leverage that gravity.

### Mistake 2: Building a marketplace but executing SaaS-like go-to-market
You can attract learners directly, but you only recruit institutions and hope learners follow. You're not leveraging your demand lever.

### Mistake 3: Not recognizing that you're in transition
Early on, you're SaaS-like (institutions drive growth). Later, you might become marketplace (learners + employers reinforce each other). If you don't recognize the shift, your go-to-market becomes stale.

### Mistake 4: Optimizing unit economics for the wrong model
SaaS-like networks optimize for: cost-per-institution, revenue-per-institution. Marketplaces optimize for: CAC (customer acquisition cost), LTV (lifetime value). If you confuse the two, you'll invest in the wrong metrics.

### Mistake 5: Assuming scale makes you a marketplace
You grew to 10,000 learners, 500 institutions, and 200 employers. You assume network effects. But if it's all institutional-driven, you're still SaaS-like. Scale doesn't create network effects; the right structure does.

## Strategic Implications

**When company context is configured:**
Apply this framework using the company's specific products, competitive landscape, and strategic decisions from the competitive-intel reference files. The framework's value increases dramatically when grounded in real competitive and strategic data rather than hypotheticals.

**To diagnose your model:**
1. Pull data on growth attribution by source
2. Calculate % from supply-side growth vs. demand-side growth
3. Test cross-side network effects
4. Determine which side is driving overall growth

**Strategic implications once you know:**
- If supply-driven: Invest in supply recruitment, partnerships, and retention
- If demand-driven: Invest in demand acquisition and marketing
- If neither is working: Your model might be neither, or the marketplace might not be timing-ready
